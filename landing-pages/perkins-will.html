The Perkins+Will Challenge

Healthcare design sits at the intersection of codes that change on independent cycles and disagree with each other

FGI updates every four years. States adopt it on their own timelines. NFPA 101 and ASHRAE 170 operate independently. A design decision compliant with one standard can be non-compliant with a state amendment to another.

üìã

FGI updates every four years with inconsistent state adoption timelines

California OSHPD enforces FGI 2022. Other states are still on FGI 2018. A firm working across multiple states needs to know exactly which edition governs each project - and which state amendments layer on top of that edition. There is no central source that tracks this for you.

‚öóÔ∏è

Life science projects require cross-referencing codes that don't reference each other

A BSL-2 research lab requires coordination across IBC occupancy classification, NFPA 45, ASHRAE 170, and the NIH Design Requirements Manual. None of these codes explicitly reference the others. The burden of cross-referencing falls entirely on the project team.

‚ôø

California Title 24 accessibility diverges from federal ADA in healthcare settings

CA Title 24 Part 2 Chapter 11B imposes stricter accessible route requirements in medical care occupancies than the base ADA Standards. An architect reading only the federal standard is designing to a lower threshold than OSHPD will enforce at permit review.

üö®

A missed FGI requirement delays Health Department approval, not just plan review

Healthcare projects go through a two-track review process - building department and Health Department. A code deficiency caught by the Health Department restarts the OSHPD review clock, not just the building permit. That delay can cost months on a project timeline where every week has a real operational cost.

For a critical care ICU in a California acute care hospital, what is the minimum patient room area required by FGI, and does OSHPD amend this?
Under FGI 2022 Section 2.2-2.1.1.1, the minimum patient room area for a critical care ICU is 200 square feet of clear floor area per bed. However, California OSHPD amends this requirement under CBC 2022 Section 1276.4: OSHPD requires a minimum of 250 square feet of clear floor area per bed, plus a mandatory 4-foot clear zone on at least three sides of each bed - a requirement that supersedes the base FGI for all projects permitted under OSHPD jurisdiction. Additionally, NFPA 101 ¬ß 18.2.2 governs the corridor width serving this unit.
